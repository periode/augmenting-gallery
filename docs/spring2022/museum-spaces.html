recap

the museum as preservation

the first role of museums is to gather artefacts deemed interesting.

-> why was a particular artefact chosen over another?

The first role of the museum is to preserve: that is, to archive and keep in ideal condition things that seem interesting to the collector. Reasons why an object might be deemed interesting can include: being part of a foreign culture, being part of a foreign time, being an unusual manifestation of a usually common object (e.g. cabinet of wonders).

The act of choosing that object already makes it interesting, and changes the semantic (meaningful) load onto that object.

The reason can be political, symbolical, technical, aesthetic, etc.

the museum as education

ways of communicating include exposure, explanation and duplication. (both for amateurs and professionals)

what kind of absent facts are being communicated through this object?

Once the artefact is chosen, and once it is shown to the public, the implicit question is "why". The reason why is that those objects, by virtue of being "originals", stand as powerful representations of a bigger concept (a culture, a time, a place, a style, etc.). As a curator or designer, what you should keep in mind are those absent facts, those things that the object stands in for.

Because these absent facts aren't immediately available, they need to be communicated. This communication can happen either by having the public confronted with the object, and the object only (exposure); it can happen by providing material and individuals who will guide the interpretation of the public (explanation); it can happen by providing the public with copies of the object that they can make their own and appreciate in different environments and under different lights.

the museum as research

the objects are the primary sources for figuring out:

  • how things were (really) done
  • how things might have been done

examples

  • ternary computers
  • fashion details
  • concrete (roman vs. contemporary)

the museum as community

however, museums are acknowledging that stories always have different sides to it, and are moving towards being more inclusive.

who are the persons in charge of the museum? are they the same that are visiting?

The more recent developments of museum studies is a consequence of the rejection of the "monolithic narrative". Because history is multi-faceted, and because the nation-states on which museums are related to are changing in terms of their demographics, museums are adapting to new publics. This includes new ways of including publics in its discourse (comments, workshops, conferences), and new ways of exhibiting those histories.

the role of architecture

do you have favorite museum buildings? why?

  • whitney, looks strange from outside, eye catching
  • studio ghibli, feels homely, building as content
  • mori art gallery, integrated in business district
  • neues national gallery, for architecture

the history between museums and buildings traces ranges from the political (the Louvre), to the artistic (the Guggenheim) and the functional (the Tate Modern).

In terms of architecture, museum buildings exist along a gradient between the political, the artistic and the functional.

The political highlights the socailly representative nature of the building, and therefore of the museum. The Louvre, former royal palace opened up to the public after the revolution, is an example in one direction, while the Uffizi galleries in Florence represent the wealth and taste of the ruling Medici family.

The artistic focuses on the building as a work of art in itself, usually designed by a leading architecture agency. The relationship is no longer to the objects, but to the new display of financial and cultural influence by states and companies. Examples of these include the Guggenheim in Abu Dhabi, the Louis Vuitton Foundation, or the Prada Foundation.

Finally, the functional is the latest stage in museum architecture, in which the spatial organization is second not to political statement, not to artistic display, but to the presentation and communication of the museum itself as a pedagogical and research institutions. Such buildings include the Dia Foundation, the Massachussetts Museum of Contemporary Art, or the Centre Pompidou.

reflective space

reflective space is one in which the linear dimension of the museum space reflects the linear dimension of the knowledge delivery.

The first type of space that muesums exhibit are reflective spaces, in which the physical building reinforces the paradigm under which the museum operates. In most cases, such paradigms can be the linear development of time in art history, the grouping of formally similar artists together, and the separation by geographical boundaries.

The picture here is from the Tate Gallery in London, and the perspective offered to you here hints at a "pearl necklace" layout, in which each small room is connected to the others by a single pathway in, and a single pathway out. The flow of the public is therefore heavily constrained, and implies a strict order in which to approach the collection.

descriptive space

descriptive space is, on the opposite, multi-dimensional. it leaves agency to the visitor to compose their own path, and assemble their own perspectives.

As a counterpoint to linear space, descriptive space is a space in which the objects of display are laid out such that they can be approached from different perspectives, and in different orders.

The itinirerary of the visitor is in this case a little less pre-determined, and the appreciation of the objects can be made on the visitor's own terms. However, studies have shown that such a descriptive approach has led to visitors spending less time per object, but viewing more objects altogether when compared with visits within reflective spaces.

The top picture is from Dia:beacon in upstate NY, and the bottom one is the Neue Nationalgalerie in Berlin. Both of these spaces contrast with spaces like the Tate, above, in that they immediately suggest multiple paths to explore the collection.

social space

along with being informational, spaces are also social. there are points of encounter where the focus isn't getting to know something else, but getting to know someone else.

Finally, the latest developments in museum studies have shifted the focus on the development of social spaces. The museum hall, courtyard or park then becomes a deliberate social hub, in which communities meet each other, rather than exclusively interacting with inanimate objects.

These two pictures illustrate some of these spaces. The first is the atrium of the Haus der Kulturen der Welt in Berlin, a neutral space which can be re-purposed as a welcoming grounds for different sorts of events and exhibitions. The second is the Cour Marly at the Louvre, taking advantadge of the existing architecture to become a movement hub, with visitors being exposed to each other more with greater intensity than in individual galleries.

exhibition design

the display as additional space

by laying out objects in space, we create a different kind of architecture, called scenography / museography.

it's the craft of translating the why of museology into the how of the exhibition.

it involves spacing, hanging, lighting and delimiting.

While buildings themselves create a spatial influence, another influence is the layout of objects within that space. We'll see how those are affected by the groupings of objects, their spacing and the relationship of those objects with the building itself.

Some interesting exhibition designs include: manus x machina at the met and the ddr museum, and the Kraftwerk.

Another one is Caryatis in Athens, waiting for their sister to come back from London.

the compound meaning of objects

kuleshov effect: assembling objects together gives them an additional layer of meaning.

we can group objects according to:

  • origin / geographical zone
  • time / time period
  • thematically
  • styles / aesthetics
  • medium / material
  • ownership
  • creator (retrospective) -> often chronological
  • gender
  • technological progress
  • culture

a collection of the same object - highlights differences (fabiola exhibition at the national portrait gallery)

the central piece of a whole museum standing out from the rest of the collection (mona lisa at the louvre)

cross-temporal, cross-cultural, but similar form (permanent exhibition at the louvre abu dhabi)

the collection as a formal art category (frederick kiesler for peggy guggenheim)

Grouping objects together gives them a different dimension, by the sheer effect of juxtaposition; not unlike a charade, multiple objects together form a sort of a charade that needs to be solved by the visitor (that charade might be more or less hard to solve).

In terms of spacing, the breathing room left around these objects, and the way they are hung tell us a lot about their status. The first example is the fabiola exhibition at the national portrait gallery, focusing on the subtle differences as markers of identity and unicity within a greater, homogeneous culture.

The second example is the Mona Lisa at the Louvre. The altar-like structure and the separation from both the audience and the rest of the collection is a testimony to the god-like status of the piece within the museum and tourism industry.

The last example is an exhibition designed by a De Stijl member, Frederick Kiesler, who focuses on the removal of the limits between works and public, and lets the canvases hang, within reach of the visitors' hands.

other categories to present collections include:

  • materials
  • influences
  • color
  • size
  • style
  • owners/collectors
  • geographic location
  • commission / self-made
  • alone / with help
  • whether the artist went to art school
  • ...

and each of these will tell a different story.

design vs. display

exhibition design starts from the space, and fills it with objects.

exhibition display starts with objects, and arranges the space to present them in the best possible light.

the highlight is either on the building, or on the collection.

Finally, it is important to keep in mind the relationship between the building and the object: which one is supposed to highlight the other? Or do they just exist in separate worlds?

Your margin of action is greater if the building is leaving some room to the object, and to the object display, rather than if you had to respect the strict physical limitations imposed by the building. Furthermore, the 'status' of the building, such as made by a starchitect, can strongly influence the reason for the visit in the first place.

visitor flows

the architecture of the museum provides one of three contexts:

  • physical context (embodied location)
  • personal context (work that is previously seen)
  • social context (social groups)

Physical context is the precise location of the visitor's body as they occupy the museum space. Where are they standing? What are they seeing? What are they hearing? Given those inputs, what kind of impressions can they get of the museum (e.g. welcoming, imposing, playful, cold, etc.)

The personal context is both long-term (cultural background, financial background, etc.) and short-term (did they just spend 4 hours in that particular museum, or did they just arrive? how often do they come to museums?)

The social context is the group (or lack of group) that they are visitors are with. Small social circle of friends? Tourist group? Private tour?

ways of visiting

  • directed (following written directions) (starting point / end point vs. checkpoints)
  • ceremonial (following someone)
  • collective (following a group)
  • constructed (following oneself)

directed is too limited/constrained for experiments, too objective/official/impersonal, not tailored to individual preferences

Each of these ways of visiting will affect:

soft/hard architectures

what is architecture?

"Architecture is the fundamental organization of a system embodied in its components, their relationships to each other, and to the environment, and the principles guiding its design and evolution." - IEEE 1471

there are as many differences between buildings and software as their are similarities.

This definition could apply both to physical and virtual architectures. We'll see in this section that architecture is a useful way to think of the environment in which our message is delivered. How do components and relations highlight an overarching principle? The organization of the environment, whether built or informational, is the common point here.

buildings as states and flows

architecture can be envisioned both as concrete states (walls, roofs, bricks, etc.), but also as flows and possibilities (walkways, platforms, mezzanines, etc.)

floorplan for the canada museum of nature

diagram of the same museum

The first image represents the floorplan of the Canada Museum of Nature, and it is unclear what spaces mean besides their immediate functionality (stairs, bathrooms). From looking at this only, one cannot tell which room is the most attractive, where do visitors spend most of their time, what is the gauge capacity (max. people in a room) and what are the connections between each room.

The second image represents the same museum, but in terms of user flows. The spaces are represented both by their practical function, but also by their roles. They are connected to one another and help us form a mental map of the possibilities offered to each visitors to craft their relatively personal tour.

software as states and flows

similarly, software can be approached by from the hard aspect, or the soft aspect.

software architecture focused on the state of the machine

software architecture focused on the flow of the human

Similarly, software architecture can denote very dry technical diagrams (such as the first image) or rather represent an application in terms of possibilities of navigation offered to the user. During the development of your app, you should focus rather on the second approach: what matters is what can the user see and do.

similarities

-> components

in buildings, components can be rooms or plazas. in software, they're scenes, or views.

-> relations

in buildings, relations can be lines of sight, hallways, or doors. in software, they're hyperlinks, or buttons, previews.

-> principle

in buildings, principles can be linear, grid-like or radial. in software, they're single-page applications, endless scroll, gyroscope-based.

Unity Scenes are the building blocks of your app, and they should be as necessary and as meaningful as a real, brick and mortar room. Each room should have a clear purpose, while leaving some room (no pun intended) to the user to possibly explore the content wihtin that room at their own pace.

In terms of relations, links and buttons do not quite match with hallways, doors and thresholds, because one cannot naturally "see" where the button leads to. Digital design principles such as preview, peek, new-tab are ways that digital designers have tried to develop to counter this inherent limitation of visibility.

difference

in software, there is movement, but there is no space. the visitor is on a page, not in a page.

as such, there should always be a call to action and/or to reaction.
