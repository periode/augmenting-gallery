situatedness in digital media

how do tools take a situation into account? and should they always try to predict as much as possible?

magic ink, brett victor

Knowing how to use the tool allows you to get into the worldview of the developer/designer; you start to apprehend things the way they apprehend things, whether it empowers or limits your abilities.

However, knowing the tool means that you also know the potential of the tool, and you can therefore make it yours. This process of making something one's own is essential to ownership; not just ownership in the material sense, but ownership of of knowledge as well. Once you've made it, you own it, and you can only own the tool if you know the tool itself, and not just its use.

This relates to epistemology, the question of where knowledge comes from. Knowledge of a thing, rather than knowledge of how to use a thing, is often more valuable.

constructive memory

human memory is constructive memory: memories are created as they are accessed.

computer memory is static memory: memories are exactly the same as they were first written.

yesterscape

Constructive memory means that we actively create memories as we recall them. While a computer, for now, is only accessing memory in a location, humans combine them to create new ones. Which means that our memories, obivously, aren't "objective", they are tailored not only to our experiences, but to how we recall these experiences. The consequence of that is that, if computers start to have constructive memories, they would start to be personalized.

three different worlds

external world

interpreted world

expected world

tools can have an influence on all of these: how so? by being a recipient for thoughts/personalities

The external world is the world that exists, the world that we start from.

The interpreted world is how we make sense of that world through pre-existing cultural knowledge, or through contextual cues.

The expected world is the world that we would like to manifest through our actions (or lack thereof). It is the result of our intention as enabled by affordances; how we change the world.

Digital applications help us in the 2nd and the 3rd worlds, traditionally: they help us interpret, and act upon the world. However, with AR, digital applications also affect the first world.

memories as the basis of the individual

constructive memories means taking into account the individual, and its current needs. technology doesn't exist in a void.

Just as we are constituted as a series of events, is it possible to apprehend computers as learning as well? To some extent, this is what AI is doing: developing recommendation algorithms happens through the recurring process of learning, presenting, learning again.


augmented space

lev manovich

lev manovich wrote the influential book the language of new media, in which he highlights the specificity of new media.

Manovich theorized the unique specificities of new media, which are:

  • numerical representation: everything can be a number
  • modularity: a system can be broken into smaller parts and replaced in different places, physical integrity is no longer a problem
  • automation: a set of rules can act on their own
  • variability: new media is customizable
  • transcoding: through numerical representation, data can be coded in different ways: e.g. data visualization

augmented places

art galleries can be places for innovation in terms of medium delivery.

which other places are the most innovative digitally, today?

A place is a space that has a purpose, a use. This means that it has a past, and a future. The art gallery is a space that is turned into a place by being appropriated, manipulated, changed by the artists, curators, etc.

Some of the places that are the most innovative today are commercial spaces: NYTimes office with a stream of text from the internet, and KFC (this in particular shows the intertwinings of the real and the cyber.

Stores and galleries innovate through private initiative. Since it is harder for museums to act on such a short term (because they're large institutions, represent tradition, public sector, etc.), they can use digital media to bypass some of these limitations.

architecture

software finds a metaphor in architecture:

  • designing places to facilitate living/thinking
  • the process of conception and construction are similar

There is a relationship in software architecture, how to organize components for it to make sense to the programmer, but there is also information architecture: how the user exists within an information space that is easy to navigate.

If there is a concrete layer from which we have to start from (whether it's actual cement, or hardware), there is a way to arrange information around it such that the user can maximize her cognitive abillities (just like architecture maximizes the ability to live).

scene management

A Unity project can be separated in different Scenes. Each scene contains its own active GameObjects and will not interfere with other scenes. Each scene must be added in the Build Settings.

using Unity.SceneManagement; //-- don't forget the namespace!

void Start(){
 //-- you can also refer to it by the number in the Build Settings
  SceneManager.LoadScene("MainScene");
}

audio

Unity can play audio given:

  • an Audio Source (which is the component that plays audio)
  • an Audio Listener (which is the component which simulates your ears)

Unity can have 3D settings for how the audio should behave.

lerping

aka Linear Interpolation, a technique to smoothly transition from one value to another.

float lerpAmount = 0f;
float lerpIncrease = 0.1f;
float myValue;

void Update(){
  myValue = (20f, 34f, lerpAmount);
  lerpAmount += lerpIncrease;
}
